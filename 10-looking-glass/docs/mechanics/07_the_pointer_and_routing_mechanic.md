# The Pointer and Routing Mechanic (Spider Jumps & The Question-First Lens)

This note defines the radical efficiency and claim-safety of the AsymX data structure.

The system does not rewrite the internet.
The system does not host content that already exists.
AsymX is not an encyclopedia; it is a relational index.

## The Core Formula

**AsymX reveals nothing as fact.**
AsymX places public traces in relation and asks what tension, direction, or recovery possibility may appear.

## The Question-First Lens

A pointer system can still become a claim-machine if it dictates what a source *means*.
To prevent this, the AsymX Lens is strictly "question-first".

- The lens does not state: "This is the problem."
- The lens does not state: "Here is friction."
- The lens asks: "What possible tension, mismatch, or transition appears when Source A and Source B are placed side-by-side?"

The lens may not lock reality; the lens must open observation.

## The 6-Step Architecture

1. **Node / Source:** A public, existing trace or URL.
2. **Relational Lens:** Observation formed as a question, never a hard conclusion.
3. **Spider Jump:** The visual or logical connection drawn between two sources.
4. **Tension Question:** "What becomes visible here?"
5. **Invitation:** "Do we want to explore this together?"
6. **Tentacle:** The temporary field of application (e.g., logistics, e-commerce, inner field) where this jump lands.

## The Open Invitation

AsymX is an open mechanism that invites areas to connect when friction, pressure, or difference wants to become visible. 

The tentacles (Sligro, Bol, Maka) are applications, not the core. Our ideas are explorations, not the system itself. 

Every source-jump must end in an invitation, leaving the responsibility of the conclusion entirely to the observer.
